The Graph (not DAG; not a Bayesian network)

Graph represents the salmon migration and birth cycle. Each edge represent a year pass. The nodes are square because they‚Äôre given. The information is given from previous knowledge.

The Models - Introducing Probability into the Graph

We‚Äôre going to take the graph that represent Salmon‚Äôs migration cycle and introduce uncertainty to it (model it via probability). By doing this we create a new graph that is complete different from the Salmon‚Äôs migration cycle graph. It is a graph base on probability view.

We go through each square node and one by one apply a model and probability to it.

Model is time base, t will represent a particular year.

  • Sp t = Sp1 t + Sp2 t = # of spawners at t-th year
  • W t = # of eggs spawned by the adults returning in year t
  • 0+ t = Young-of-the-year at t-th year
  • PSm t = pre-smolts at t-th year
  • Sm1 t = 1+ smolts (1 year to smolt) at t-th year
  • P1 t = Parr1 = smaller juveniles left behind by Sm1 at t-th year
  • Sm2 t = 2+ smolts (2 years to smolt) at t-th year

Spawners -> Eggs

W t = Sp t ‚ãÖ P_f ‚ãÖ fec

  • W t = # of eggs spawned by the adults returning in year t
  • Sp t = # of spawners = Sp1 + Sp2
  • P_f = proportion of females
  • fec = mean of fecundity (fertility)

Eggs -> 0+ juveniles

This is Ricker Cruve model with parameters (Œ±,Œ≤) which is a classic discrete population model.

0+t+1 = Œ± ‚ãÖ Wt ‚ãÖ e-Œ≤ ‚ãÖ Wt ‚ãÖ eŒµt where Œµt ~iid N(0, œÉ2)

  • 0+t+1 = freshwater production of juveniles resulting from the reproduction of the spawners returning in year t

0+ juveniles -> Smolts

PSmt+2 ~ Binomial(0+t+1, Œ≥0+) = # of 0+t+1 will survive and migrate to PSmt+2

Sm1t+2 ~ Binomial(PSmt+2, Œ∏Sm1) = # of PSmt+2 will survive and migrate as 1+smolts (Sm1)

Sm2t+3 ~ Binomial(Parr1t+2, Œ≥parr1) = # of Parr1t+2 will survive and migrate as 2+smolts (Sm2)

  • PSmt+2 = young-of-the-year 0+t+1 will survive next spring year t+2
  • Œ≥0+ = survival rate of 0+
  • Œ∏Sm1 = proportion of pre-smolts will migrate as 1+Smolts (survival rate)
  • Œ≥parr1 = survival rate of parr1

Sp1t+3 ~ Binomial(Sm1t+2, Œ≥Sm)

Sp2t+4 ~ Binomial(Sm2t+3, Œ≥Sm)

  • Œ≥Sm = survival rate

These two are observed and given:

Using these data points we can figure out the unknowns.

Our unknowns, the parameters, are: Œ±, Œ≤, œÉ, Œ≥0+, Œ∏sm1, Œ≥Parr1, Œ≥Sm

# of smolts caught downstream trap can be model as a binomial distribution either the smolt is caught or not.

Okay so what? Where‚Äôs the Bayesian network?

Not yet. The book needs to introduce the concept of a simple model vs a hierarchical model and some terminology.

So far we haven‚Äôt introduce any observational variable (random variable) at all.

  • Œ∏ represents parameters
  • Z represents latent parameters
  • Y represents the output Random Variable. (little y represent the realization/sample of Y random variable).

Left is a simple model. The right graph is a hierarchical model.

Z represents latent variables (nuisance variables), basically variables we don‚Äôt really care, also they‚Äôre hidden we don‚Äôt observed it directly like Y. Y represents observations. Observations are random so Y is capitalized and smaller y is the realization of Y or a sample of Y. The node is pink because it is an observable.

P[Œ∏, Z, Y] = P[Œ∏] ‚ãÖ P[Z | Œ∏] ‚ãÖ P[Y | Œ∏, Z]

Well first what‚Äôs the experiment?

For this it‚Äôs salmon captures and they‚Äôre model via binomial distribution either you catch the fish or not.

Notice the C stands for catches.

  • C0+, t+1 ~ Binomial(0+t+1, œÄ0+)
  • CSm1, t+2 ~ Binomial(Sm1t+2, œÄSm)
  • CSm2, t+3 ~ Binomial(Sm1t+3, œÄSm)
  • CSp1, t+3 ~ Binomial(Sp1t+3, œÄSp)
  • CSp2, t+4 ~ Binomial(Sp2t+4, œÄSp)

Once again the squares represent known/given values (the œÄ‚Äôs are given). The pink circle means observed values. Pink in general means they‚Äôre known either by given or by observations. The purple boxes represent grouping and group the nodes into their respective group.

Ok. Finally, we got a Bayesian network. Really, what now?

How does y (the sample or realization of Y) fits in this fancy graph?

What happen when the Observation is available?

Before that notice how we build the model and the direction. The direction is downward from the Salmon cycle toward the latent variable and then towards the obsevation.

Why did the book brought this up? It is because when you train the model using the data/observations that are available you go in the opposite direction.

You start at the Y (observation layer and Y is a random variable) and Y is now, Y = y, since little y is the realization of random variable Y. y is a sample of Y or the data (values not just some placeholder variable). And you go up to latent layer and then to the parameter later.

Let‚Äôs see it mathematically:

Here‚Äôs the joint probability:

P[Y, Œ∏, Z]

Now here‚Äôs the joint probability with Y = y, when we have data to train the model and find the paramenter.

P[Œ∏, Z | Y = y]

Given Y = y, the observations propagate upward from the observation to the latent layer to the parameter layer.

This is how you train the model after you are done creating the model.

You can see the Bayes Rule connection too right? We‚Äôre always dealing with Joint Probability and Conditional Probability.

Bayesian make it so that they‚Äôre conditionally independent. This is one of the property of Bayesian statistic.

This is now a posterior distribution. Posterior being after the data. Prior distribution is before the data.

P[Œ∏, Z | Y = y] = posterior distributionundefined

I‚Äôm going to repeat it again.

Posterior is after the data have been inputed.

Prior is before the data. It is your prior belief.

In Bayesian you need to supply a belief in form of a prior distribution. It‚Äôs weird but don‚Äôt worry if you don‚Äôt know anything then you can use a noninformative prior distribution.

The belief thing is also away to encode expert belief too.

So given what we have now, we just have to apply Bayes‚Äô Rule to the conditional probability and you get your parameter values.

Bayes‚Äô Rule

P[Œ∏ Z | Y = y] = P[Œ∏, Z, Y = y] / P[Y = y]

Some stat here and you get.

P[Œ∏ Z | Y = y] ‚àù P[Œ∏] ‚ãÖ P[Z | Œ∏] ‚ãÖ P[Y = y | Œ∏, Z]
